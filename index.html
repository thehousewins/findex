The Verdict: Unconscionable Conduct

The most recent and damning chapter involves the NSW Supreme Court's finding against Findex chair Terry Paule in the Botanical Water case. This section breaks down the court's findings and the timeline of events that led to a nearly $16M USD judgment against Mr. Paule for his treatment of an inventor and his partner.

"The court found Melbourne businessman Terry Paule engaged in 'unconscionable conduct', and that he abused his position in a bottled water business..."

- Finding by Justice Michael Ball, NSW Supreme Court

Timeline of a Takeover

An Inventor's Struggle

Dr. Bruce Kambouris, living in a factory room, develops a process to create clean drinking water from vegetable waste. His partner, David Driver, loans the company over $680,000.

The Paules Enter

Terry and Spiro Paule, through their company MyCo, demand a 50% stake in Aqua Botanical without any initial investment, a proposal the court deemed "unacceptable."

Pressure and Control

Terry Paule agrees to fund the company but then refuses, using what the court noted as coercive language. The Paules' company, MyCo, takes over management.

The IP is Stripped

A complex restructure, with "administrative heavy lifting" by Findex, moves the intellectual property to a UK entity. Dr. Kambouris and Mr. Driver are not appointed directors, despite assurances.

Dilution and Deception

A capital raise dilutes the founders to a minority stake. The Paules award themselves shares at a fraction of the price paid by other investors.

Justice Served

The court finds Terry Paule breached his fiduciary duties and engaged in "unconscionable conduct," ordering a payment of over $15.8M USD to the original partners.

A Pattern of Practice

The Findex Question

Terry and Spiro Paule are the Chairman and CEO of Findex, one of Australia's largest financial advisory firms, managing the wealth and trust of countless clients.

The court's finding of "unconscionable conduct," coupled with a history of controversial dealings, casts a long shadow over their leadership.

Are these men, with such a documented history, fit to hold office? And should Findex clients be worried that the same disregard for others' interests could impact the integrity of the firm they lead?
